1. Objective

Build a Progressive Growing GAN (ProGAN) to generate realistic facial images, following the architecture from Karras et al. (ICLR 2018) and the Paperspace implementation guide. The model is trained on a subset of 10,000 CelebA face images and progressively grows from 4×4 to 128×128 resolution.

2. Architecture Overview

2.1 Progressive Growing

The key innovation of ProGAN is progressive training: both the generator and discriminator start at a very low resolution (4×4) and gradually add new layers to double the resolution at each stage. This approach has two main advantages:

  • Stability: Training starts with simple, low-frequency structures (overall face shape, color) before tackling fine details (hair texture, skin pores).
  • Speed: Most of training time is spent at low resolutions where computation is cheap.

2.2 Network Components

ComponentPurpose
Equalized Learning RateWeights initialized N(0,1), scaled at runtime by He constant √(2/fan_in). Ensures all layers learn at the same speed.
Pixel NormalizationNormalizes each pixel’s feature vector to unit length. Replaces batch normalization in the generator for more stable training.
Minibatch Std DevAppends a statistical summary of the minibatch as an extra feature map in the discriminator, encouraging output diversity.
Fade-in (Alpha Blending)When a new resolution block is added, it is linearly blended in using α that ramps from 0 → 1 over the first half of training at that stage.

3. Upsampling Strategy

Choice: Nearest-Neighbor Upsampling + Learned Convolutions
Instead of using transposed convolutions (which can cause checkerboard artifacts), ProGAN uses nearest-neighbor interpolation followed by standard 3×3 convolutions. This is a deliberate design decision from the original paper that produces smoother upsampling with fewer artifacts.

In the discriminator, the corresponding downsampling is done with average pooling (AvgPool2d), which provides a smooth, non-parameterized reduction in spatial dimensions — symmetric to the upsampling approach.

5. Results Description

5.1 Early Stages (4×4 to 16×16)

At the lowest resolutions, the generator quickly learns the basic color palette of faces — skin tones, rough face shape, and background contrast. Images at 4×4 are essentially colored blobs, but by 16×16, one can begin to distinguish the oval face shape, hair regions, and approximate eye/mouth positions.

5.2 Mid Stages (32×32 to 64×64)

This is where facial features become recognizable. At 32×32, eyes, nose, and mouth are discernible as distinct regions. By 64×64, the generated faces show clear facial structure with identifiable features like hair style, skin color variation, and basic symmetry. The fade-in mechanism ensures smooth transitions — without it, adding a new block would cause a sudden quality drop.

5.3 Final Stage (128×128)

At the highest resolution, the model produces faces with visible detail: hair texture, eye color, lip definition, and even background variation. With only 10,000 training images and limited epochs, some artifacts may remain (slight asymmetry, blurry regions), but the progressive approach ensures the overall structure is coherent.

5.4 Training Dynamics

The Wasserstein loss with gradient penalty provides a meaningful training signal. Key observations across epochs:

  • Discriminator loss starts negative (correctly separating real/fake) and stabilizes near zero as the generator improves.
  • Generator loss decreases as generated images become more realistic.
  • The fade-in period (α ramping) shows temporary loss spikes as the network adjusts to the new resolution, followed by stabilization.
  • At higher resolutions, training per epoch is slower but each epoch covers fewer passes over the data (smaller batch sizes).

6. Key Implementation Decisions

DecisionRationale
PyTorch instead of TF/KerasEnvironment has PyTorch 2.11 available; dynamic computation graph simplifies progressive architecture changes
WGAN-GP lossMore stable than vanilla GAN loss; gradient penalty enforces Lipschitz constraint without weight clipping
Nearest-neighbor upsampleAvoids checkerboard artifacts from transposed convolutions (per original ProGAN paper)
Fresh optimizers per stagePrevents momentum accumulation from previous resolution from destabilizing new layers
10,000 image subsetBalances computational cost with sufficient variety for face generation
